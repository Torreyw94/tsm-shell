♡ LIFE INSURANCE 6 rebuttals ▾
"I already have life insurance through work — I don't need more."
VERY COMMON
"That's a great benefit to have — and it's a real starting point. Here's the problem: most group policies are 1–2× your salary, and the DIME method shows most families need 10–12× income. If you earn $80,000 your employer might provide $80,000–$160,000 in coverage — but your family may need $900,000+.

There's also a portability problem. The moment you leave that job — voluntarily or not — that coverage disappears overnight. A personal policy is yours forever regardless of your employer. Group life is a starting point, not a finish line. Can I run the DIME numbers for you right now so you can see your actual gap?"
💡 Run the DIME calculator live. The gap between their employer coverage and the real number is the close.
"I can't afford life insurance right now."
PRICE
"I completely hear you — budgets are tight. But let me put this in perspective. A healthy 35-year-old can get $500,000 in term life coverage for about $28 a month — less than a streaming service. That's 93 cents a day to protect your family from losing everything.

Now consider the flip side: if something happened to you tomorrow, could your family afford the mortgage, the kids' education, the debts — all without your income? The time you truly can't afford life insurance is after you need it. Right now you're healthy and insurable — that's the lowest rate you'll ever qualify for. Should we see what a policy would actually cost you per day?"
💡 Convert premium to a daily cost ($28/mo = $0.93/day). Nobody can say they can't afford less than a dollar a day.
"I'm young and healthy — I don't need it yet."
AGE
"Being young and healthy is exactly why NOW is the perfect time — not later. Life insurance pricing is based on age and health. The rate you qualify for today is the lowest you'll ever see. Every year you wait, the premium rises. And if your health changes — diabetes, high blood pressure, a diagnosis — you could become uninsurable or pay 2–3× more.

You're also likely to get married, buy a home, have kids — your need is only going to grow. Lock in your low rate now while your health is an asset you can leverage. What does the next 5 years of your life look like?"
💡 Have them project their life forward 5 years — future responsibilities make the need self-evident.
"I need to think about it / talk to my spouse."
DELAY
"Absolutely — this is a family decision and your spouse should be part of it. Is there a specific concern they might have that I can help you address now, so you're not having the same conversation twice?

Here's what I'd suggest: let's start the application today — no payment, no obligation until it's approved and you've both reviewed it. Every policy includes a free-look period where you can return it for a full refund if you decide it's not right. That way you and your spouse can review the actual policy together, ask me any questions, and make the decision with full information. Does that work?"
💡 Starting the application creates commitment. The free-look period removes risk and gets a signature today.
"Whole life is a bad investment — buy term and invest the difference."
SOPHISTICATE
"You've done your research — and for a disciplined investor who actually invests the difference consistently for 30 years, term can win on pure return math. I won't argue that.

But whole life isn't competing with your Roth IRA or index funds — it's filling a different role. The cash value is tax-deferred, creditor-protected in Arizona under ARS §20-1131, has no contribution limits, and carries guaranteed death benefit regardless of market performance. For business owners or high earners who've maxed their tax-advantaged accounts, whole life becomes a powerful tax shelter — not an investment vehicle. What does your current tax-advantaged situation look like?"
💡 Never fight the term vs. whole debate — position them for different roles. Ask about their tax situation to find the real need.
"I'm retired — my kids are grown, I don't need life insurance."
RETIREE
"That makes complete sense at first glance. But let me ask: do you have a surviving spouse who depends on your Social Security or pension? When you pass, do those income streams reduce or stop? What happens to their lifestyle on 60% of the income?

Also consider: final expenses in Arizona — funeral, burial, estate settlement — average $15,000–$25,000. And life insurance is one of the most tax-efficient ways to pass wealth to children or grandchildren — the death benefit transfers income-tax-free. A final expense policy can cover immediate costs and leave something meaningful, often for $50–$100 a month. Would that be worth exploring?"
💡 Pivot to surviving spouse income loss and estate transfer efficiency — these land strongly with retirees.
✚ HEALTH / ACA 4 rebuttals ▾
"I'm healthy — I'll just pay the penalty if I don't have insurance."
COMMON
"There's no federal penalty anymore — you're right. But here's the real math: the average ER visit in Arizona is $2,400. A broken arm runs $7,000–$18,000. An appendectomy is $15,000–$30,000 out of pocket. One unexpected event can wipe out years of savings.

What most people in your situation don't know is that with your income you may qualify for Premium Tax Credits that make a plan essentially free — or very low cost. I've seen clients get a Silver plan with a $150 deductible for under $50 a month after APTC. Give me 5 minutes to run your actual numbers — then you can make a fully informed decision."
💡 Run the APTC calculator immediately — showing near-free coverage is the most powerful rebuttal to this objection.
"The deductible is too high — I'll never be able to use it."
DEDUCTIBLE
"That's one of the most valid frustrations with health insurance today.
